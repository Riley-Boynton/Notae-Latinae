Nouns

Nouns in Latin are the same as nouns in English: a person, place, thing, or idea. Adjectives also take the form of nouns and decline like so as well. Adjectives must match the noun in case, gender, and number.
Noun Endings
Download the chart
Nominative

Uses:

1. Subject of Sentence: e.g. Caecilius est pater. [Caecilius is the dad.]

2. Complete verb to be: e.g. Caecilius est pater. [Caecilius is the dad.]

3. Apposition (specify another noun): e.g. Hannibal, dux Carthaginiensis. [Hannibal, the king of the Cartheginians.]

4. Exclamation: e.g. O tempora! [Oh the times!]

Genitive

Uses:

1. Possession: e.g. Polyspaston Haterii. [Haterius’s crane.]

2. Partitive—describing part of something: e.g. Nemo virorum. [None of the men.]

3. Description: e.g. Vir magnae virtutis. [A man of great virtue.]

(Just generally the word that follows “of”)

Dative

Uses:

1. Indirect object: e.g. Magister discipulis librum dat. [The teacher gave the students a book.]

2. Purpose: e.g. Auxilio misit. [He sent them as help.]

3. Reference: e.g. Haec tibi scribo. [I am writing this for you.]

4. Direct object of verbs: parere, placere, favere, nocere, persuadere: e.g. Mihi placet. [It pleases me.]

5. Subject in passive periphrastic: e.g. Mihi effugiendum est! [I must flee!]

Accusative

Uses:

1. Direct object: e.g. Ego pecuniam habeo. [I have money.]

2. With prepositions ad (to), in (into), per (through), sub (up to): e.g. Grumio per culinam ambulat. [Grumio walks through the kitchen.]

Ablative

Uses:

1. Prepositions sub (below), in (in), de, sine, pro, a/ab, cum, e/ex; Mnemonic: SID SPACE: e.g. Caecilius est in horto. [Caecilius is in the garden.]

2. Ablative of Manner—usually has preposition cum: e.g. Puer in silvis cum amico ambulat. [The boy walks in the woods with a friend.]

3. Ablative of Means—answers the question “by which means did ___ happen?”: e.g. Cogidubnus testamentum stilo scripsit. [Cogidubnus wrote his will with his stylus.]

4. Ablative of Separation—when you are freed from something: e.g. Clemens servitute liberabatur. [Clemens was freed from slavery.]

5. Ablative of Time—expresses time when something occurs: e.g. Nocte dormimus. [We sleep at night.]

6. Ablative Absolute—what happens relating to something else (usually noun + participle): e.g. Urbe capta, milites discesserunt. [After the city was captured, the soldiers left.]

Vocative

Uses:

1. Saying someone’s name to their face: e.g. Me Quinte! [My Quintus!]

Adjectives

Adjectives decline like nouns, and agree in gender, number, and case—but not necessarily declension or ending.

e.g. Salvium scelestum punire. [To punish the wicked Salvius.]

Supine

This is something really rare, and it comes in versions I (accusative) and II (ablative)

Supine I: expresses purpose on verbs expressing motion, comes after the verb, uses the 4th principal part, ends in -um, usually only used with venire, ire, mittere: e.g. Venit spectatum ludos. [He came for the purpose of watching the games.]

Supine II: specifies adjectives, says what the adjective applies to, also uses the 4th principal part, ends in -u, generally only used if it is really stylized: e.g. Facile dictu, difficile factu. [Easy to say, hard to do.]

Adverbs

Adverbs are just what they are in English: words that describe verbs and other adjectives. However, they don’t all end in -ly, like English. If only it were that simple!
From Adjectives

Adverbs from adjectives are the most common, and are considered the “regular” adverbs. They have a set of rules for endings, like everything else in Latin.

Positive is just the normal form: [adjective]ly, comparative is just that: [adjective]er, and Superlative is like adding “very” or “really” to something: very [adjective]ly

Grammar

These are things that people get confused about easily, or just some helpful things that I hope you won’t forget. Good luck with some of this stuff.
Dictionary Entry

A dictionary entry is what a noun or verb looks like in a dictionary. If you are using the digital version of the book, click here for the link to an online dictionary. Otherwise you can use the short one at the end of this book.

Nouns: the dictionary entry has the nominative singular, the genitive singular, then the gender, and then finally the definition. Using the genitive singular, you can find all endings by removing the genitive ending and replacing it with the ending you want: e.g. ludus, ludi, m. — game

Verbs: the dictionary entry has the first person singular present active indicative, followed by the present active infinitive, followed by the first person singular perfect active indicative, followed by the perfect passive participle (if the verb you are looking for has a PPP), and then finally the definition of the infinitive without the “to.” Once again, you can remove the endings and find all the different forms of the verbs by using the stems: e.g. amo, amare, amavi, amatus — love

Adjectives: the dictionary entry has the masculine nominative singular, followed by feminine, followed by neuter, then the definition: e.g. laetus, -a, -um — happy

Adverbs: the dictionary entry has just the word because adverbs do not decline: e.g. celeriter — quickly

Prepositions: the dictionary entry has the word, plus the case the word it affects takes: e.g in (+ abl.) — in

Other Words: the dictionary entry has just the word and definition: e.g. et — and

Purpose Clauses

These clauses express purpose. Duh. but also they are used as an indirect clause with ut or ne, which means that the following verb takes the subjunctive. These clauses tell you that something happens, followed by why something happens.

Ut is used when the expression is positive and ne is used when it is negative: e.g. Legi ut discam. [I read so that I might learn.]; e.g. Feci ne errarent. [I did it so that they would not make a mistake.]

You can tell if it is a purpose clause if it has ne.

Result Clauses

These clauses express result, also in an indirect way with ut—but not ne. If it is negative, then we use ut non instead. However, if both clauses are negative (shown in the third example) then we use quin instead of either of them! Again, because it is indirect, it is in the subjunctive.

It also usually has the signal words: tam, ita, tantus, talis, adeo: e.g Tam fessus erat ut dormiret. [He was so tired that he slept.]; e.g. Tam fessus erat ut ambulāre nōn posset. [He was so tired that he could not walk.]; e.g. Nihil tam difficile est quin strenue laborando disci possit. [Nothing is so difficult that it cannot be learned with hard work.]

Indirect Commands

This is like an imperative but you are saying that someone else said that. This is great for putting the blame on someone else. Once again in the subjunctive. Once again uses ut or ut non. But now it uses some sort of command verb at the start such as orare, rogare, or imperare: e.g. Legatus militibus imperavit ut redirent. [The commander ordered the soldiers to go back.]

Indirect Questions

These are really similar to indirect commands; both have the subjunctive, and both use verbs like rogare and other words. But indirect commands have something different: question words. These words, like quis, quid, ubi, cur, quomodo, quantus, quando, quot, and utrum, take the place of ut or ne and are the defining difference between indirect commands and questions: e.g. Quaerit quis veniat. [He asks who is coming.]

Passive Periphrastic

These are far too common, and honestly are a lot less scary than they sound and look like. This grammar construct expresses necessity, and unlike most things in Latin, it’s rather simple: a dative noun, followed by the only use for a gerundive, followed by the verb “est.” The proper translation would technically be “It must be [verb]ed by [noun],” but we usually translate it as “[noun] must [verb],” which just sounds better in English: e.g. Mihi effugiendum est! [I must flee!]; e.g. Tibi neciendum est! [You must die!]

This is a great example of a grammar construct in Latin that has thankfully faded out of use in favor of a more simple phrasing. There are also other ways of expressing necessity in Latin, but those are far less common.

Ablative Absolutes

These are hands down one of the hardest things Latin throws at you. Right when you think you have the sentence translated perfectly, you run into one of these. At least this time they didn’t name it something fancy. It is just an ablative noun and participle, absolute by itself in a sentence. It is used to express something happening at a time relative to another. The way you build an ablative absolute is simple: noun, then participle, both in ablative case. But wait! Sometimes, instead of a participle, you need another noun, or an adjective. But pretty much, if you see a couple ablatives next to each other, and they are separated from the rest of the sentence with a comma, then you probably just met an ablative absolute: e.g. Urbe capta, milites discesserunt [With the city having been captured, the soldiers left.]; e.g. Caesare duce, exercitus Romanus contra Gallos pugnavit. [While Caesar was the leader, the Roman army fought against the Gauls.]

Rules of Gender

The rules of Gender are pretty simple: 1st declension nouns can only be feminine, 2nd declension can be masculine or neuter, 3rd declension can be all three, but 4th declension can be m. or n., and 5th declension is almost always feminine, but there are some masculine nouns. You can tell if something is neuter based on the dictionary definition, or by seeing a nominative or accusative in the text.

Neuter Rule: the nominative and accusative are always the same, and in the plural they end in -a.
